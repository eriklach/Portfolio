The
Problem

Professional tools are powerful. They're also intimidating.

"Users shouldn't need a manual to find a button."

The Context

Autodesk's 3D modelling tools are industry-standard but notoriously dense. New users faced a steep learning curve, and even experienced users struggled to discover features outside their established workflows.

The result: high support ticket volume, low feature adoption, and frustrated users.

The Gap

Existing tooltip copy was either missing entirely or written by engineers — technically accurate, but not written for humans learning a new skill under time pressure.

There was an opportunity to use clear, contextual microcopy to teach users in the moment, without pulling them out of their workflow.

The Hypothesis

If we replace generic or missing tooltip copy with clear, contextual, action-oriented microcopy — written for the user's current task, not a feature spec — we can reduce friction and support load while increasing feature adoption.

Research
& Audit

You can't write better copy without understanding what's broken.

Content Audit
  • Inventory: Catalogued every existing tooltip across the target feature set — noting what was present, missing, or misleading.
  • Pattern Analysis: Identified the most common failure modes: jargon-heavy labels, vague descriptions, and tooltips that restated the button name.
  • Support Data: Cross-referenced with support tickets to prioritise which tools caused the most confusion.
User Research

Observed users during onboarding sessions to understand how they encountered the software for the first time and where they paused, backtracked, or gave up.

Key finding: users rarely read — they scan for confirmation that they're about to do the right thing.

Writing
& Design

Microcopy that earns its one line of space.

Writing Principles
  • Lead with the outcome: Tell users what this tool does for them, not what it is.
  • Plain language first: Replace technical jargon with words a new user would actually know.
  • One idea per tooltip: If a feature needs two sentences to explain, the UX might be the problem.
Collaboration

Worked closely with the product and engineering teams to ensure accuracy, and with the design team to validate that copy fit within the visual constraints of each tooltip component.

Iterated on copy through rounds of review with subject matter experts, balancing technical precision with plain-language clarity.

Style Guidelines Established
  • Format: Verb-led labels ("Extrude face") + one-sentence description of outcome, not mechanism.
  • Length: Maximum 15 words for the description. If it needed more, flag for UX review.
  • Tone: Direct and confident — like a knowledgeable colleague, not a manual.

Results
& Impact

The Outcome

After shipping the first phase of revised tooltips, the team saw a measurable reduction in support tickets related to the affected feature set, and an increase in feature adoption among new users completing onboarding.

The style guidelines established during this project became the foundation for Autodesk's broader microcopy standards.

Key Learnings
  • Words are UX: The right copy at the right moment can eliminate the need for onboarding entirely.
  • Data-driven writing: Support ticket analysis was the most valuable input for prioritising what to write first.
  • Standards compound: Building a style guide meant each subsequent phase shipped faster and more consistently.
