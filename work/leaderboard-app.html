The
Challenge

A hard deadline, a small window, and a real audience watching.

"There is no 'ship it later' when the competition starts Friday."

The Brief

An organiser of a CrossFit-style competition needed a live leaderboard that could display athlete rankings in real time as scores were entered during the event.

The existing solution was a shared spreadsheet projected on a screen — functional, but not the experience competitors and spectators deserved.

The Constraints

Two weeks from brief to event. One developer (me). The organiser needed to be able to enter scores themselves during the competition without any technical knowledge.

It had to work reliably on the day — no downtime, no data loss, no confusing UI under the pressure of a live event.

The Approach

Rather than building from scratch with traditional methods, I used an AI-first workflow — using Claude as a development partner to move through design, architecture decisions, and implementation faster than I could alone. Every line of code was reviewed and understood before it shipped.

The AI-First
Workflow

Using AI to compress the design-to-code loop.

How I Used AI
  • Architecture: Used Claude to evaluate data structure options and decide on a real-time database approach before writing any code.
  • Implementation: Worked through React components and state management with AI assistance, focusing my attention on product decisions rather than boilerplate.
  • Debugging: When issues arose, AI dramatically shortened the diagnostic loop.
What AI Didn't Replace

The design decisions, the UX thinking, and the judgment calls were all mine. AI accelerated execution — it didn't replace the product instincts that determine what to build in the first place.

Understanding every piece of code before it shipped wasn't optional — it was the job. AI made it faster, not automatic.

Design
& Build

Two interfaces, two very different users.

The Display View

Designed for a projector screen in a loud, bright gym. High contrast, large type, animated rank changes to make score updates feel dramatic and alive.

Athletes and spectators needed to read it from 30 feet away — the same visual design constraint as the WOD Timer.

The Admin View

Designed for a non-technical organiser entering scores on a tablet at a chaotic event. Large tap targets, confirmation steps to prevent accidental edits, and an undo function for score corrections.

The organiser needed zero training to use it on the day.

Technical Decisions
  • Real-time sync: Used a real-time database so score updates on the admin tablet appeared on the display screen within milliseconds — no refresh required.
  • Offline resilience: Built in local state fallback so a brief WiFi dropout wouldn't freeze the leaderboard mid-competition.
  • Simple deployment: Hosted on a static host with a simple URL the organiser could bookmark — no app installation, no logins for the display screen.

Results
& Reflection

On the Day

The app ran without a single issue across a full day of competition. The organiser entered scores independently without any support from me, and athletes gathered around the display screen between events to track their standings.

It shipped on time — which in this case was the only metric that truly mattered.

On AI-First Development
  • Speed is real: The workflow genuinely compressed a 6-week project into 2 weeks without sacrificing quality.
  • Judgment is still the job: Every AI suggestion required evaluation. The loop is faster, but the decisions are still yours.
  • This is the future: Solo designers and developers who learn to work this way will punch far above their weight class.
